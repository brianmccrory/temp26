# Cash 
date: 2023-11-02

In Japan, cash (Yen) is the most reliable payment method. US dollars are not accepted anywhere as far as I know.

Some smaller places don't accept cards (but this is changing, and lots of shops are even using apps like "Pay Pay".) 

My experience is that I love to use cash (Japanese yen) everywhere because it's so simple, convenient, and reliable.

I use cards for things like hotels/rent, airlines, and sometimes purchases at department stores or "official" places like that. At restaurants and bars I almost always use cash.

## Converting Cash Rates

In the old days, it was easy to roughly think of *100 Yen* == *1 US Dollar*. So if something costs 500 yen, it was easy to think of it as $5, or 1000 yen is about $10, etc. 

These days, the yen is very weak so it's more like *150 Yen* == *1 US Dollar*. So if something costs 500 yen today, it's easy to think of it as $3.50, or 1000 yen is about $7, etc. This has been great for foreign travelers to Japan recently, in terms of buying power. 

But I have heard rumors recently that the Japanese government is going to take some steps to try to strengthen the yen soon, which could affect the dollar to yen conversion rate soon, meaning the buying power of foreign visitors would decrease compared to how strong it has been the last few years. Who knows?

It makes things easier to get some Japanese yen in cash before you get to the airport or before you get to Japan. You can use an ATM machine once you arrive (but depending on your bank, your card, and the particular machine you use, you may have unexpected problems, which is a headache if you don't have any cash on hand.)

Right now (11/2/2023) the US Dollar to Yen rate is very favorable, and at times like these I like to use my bank's website to order Yen in cash. This is because I don't know what the rate will be when I go to Japan later on, and it may not be this good again. It's like at a 10-year high.

If I run out of cash in Japan and I need more, I just use my regular bank debit card to withdraw yen at certain ATMs in Japan. Whether or not this works for you may depend on your particular bank (in the old days, I used to call my bank and tell them I was going to be using the cards in Japan, and they would set something up in their system, but I don't know if this is still necessary these days. You should probably check with your bank.) I have no problems with my Wells Fargo debit card or my Capital One debit card to withdraw money from ATMs, but there's a limit, and there's usually a fee for using the ATM with foreign cards (plus whatever fees or conversion rates your specific bank may apply).
